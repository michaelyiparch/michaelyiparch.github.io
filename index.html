About

I'm Michael Yip (Yip Hoi Kwong), a Year 3 architecture student at Chu Hai College of Higher Education in Hong Kong. My work focuses on high-density urban housing, modular construction systems, and environmental performance — exploring how architecture can reconcile the extreme density of Hong Kong with the fundamental human need for air, light, and community.

My design approach is research-driven: I use CFD wind simulation, modular logic, and parametric thinking to develop projects that are technically grounded and spatially ambitious. I work across Revit, Rhino, Grasshopper, and Adobe Suite to produce work that communicates clearly at every scale.

Featured Project

The Aero-Matrix

High-Rise Porosity System · San Sau Street, Tuen Mun, Hong Kong

Analysis

Urban Suffocation:
A Hong Kong Story

Hong Kong's high-density urban fabric suffers from the "Wall Effect," where massive building footprints block natural air circulation. This traps heat and degrades air quality, significantly affecting neighborhood livability.

The project tackles this through Modular Integrated Construction (MiC), moving construction into controlled factory environments for maximum efficiency and precision — reducing material waste, carbon emissions, and on-site disruption.

"By combining MiC technology with a strategic grid, the building is transformed into a breathing matrix — restoring natural ventilation to the urban environment."
Site Analysis

Urban Morphology &
Multi-Level Density

The site at San Sau Street, Tuen Mun sits within narrow 'street canyons' and extreme vertical development. I analyzed building footprints at three key heights: 8m, 12m, and 20m.

While the ground level is crowded, the vertical analysis reveals a change in density as we move upward. At the 12m level, simulations show a significant environmental opening — the city has 'pockets' of wind that can be captured if the building is shaped correctly.

Environmental Strategy

CFD Analysis &
Design Response

CFD simulation revealed a strong wind corridor at 12m. The building is split into three functional zones based on height:

0m–8m (The Shield): A glass-clad podium acts as a physical shield, protecting the street from direct turbulent wind and ensuring pedestrian comfort.

8m–12m (The Portal): A 4-meter open-air terrace catches the wind corridor — the building's 'waist' allows fresh air to flow through rather than blocking it.

12m–57m (The Matrix): The Aero-Matrix system uses modular, permeable design to let air pass through the living units.

Research

Case Study: Structural Framework & MiC Logic

Park View Village

Architect: Space · Newcastle, UK · 2016–2018 · 9 Floors · Student Dormitory

This case study informed the core structural strategy of the Aero-Matrix by highlighting the separation between "permanent" and "adaptable" elements.

The design adopts a permanent central core for stability and vertical circulation, while utilizing MiC for the residential units. By treating the MiC modules as independent "plug-in" components, the system allows for high-precision off-site manufacturing and flexible on-site assembly.

"This separation of permanent and adaptable elements directly enables the empty slots within the grid — the lungs of the building."
Concept

From Genetic Unit to Aero-Matrix

A four-step design methodology for breathing architecture

01
Unit Prototype

A "Genetic Unit" whose geometry is shaped to minimize wind resistance and maximize natural airflow.

02
Symmetric Coupling

Two units joined by mirroring to form a shared core — creating geometric stability and spatial rhythm.

03
Systematic Assembly

Modules stacked with intentional gaps — "Airflow Porosity" that acts as lungs for the building.

04
Aero-Matrix Formation

The final matrix calibrated to Tuen Mun's site-specific winds — a "Breathable Filter" for passive cooling.

Structure

Aero-Matrix: Core & Module Integration

The Aero-Matrix uses a central core to support a system of prefabricated modules. This structural separation makes the building lighter and enables better natural ventilation.

The core — containing lifts, stairs, and services — acts as the permanent structural spine. Around it, MiC modules are arranged as independent plug-in components, allowing empty slots to be strategically placed for airflow.

This approach combines the efficiency of modular construction with the environmental performance of a porous building envelope.

Atmosphere

Three Scales of Living

Modular Void: Urban Porosity

The hollow module is a key innovation — by leaving strategic spaces empty in the building grid, we create "breathing pockets" that let wind flow through. These open modules create a "porous" building that helps cool the city, transforming structural gaps into meaningful community spaces.

Communal Heart: The Social Matrix

Located within the upper levels, this communal zone integrates a lounge and open-plan kitchen into the modular grid. Situated adjacent to the hollow MiC modules, the interior benefits from continuous cross-ventilation — merging social interaction with passive environmental performance.

Private Unit: Modular Living

Each residential module is designed for comfort and efficiency within a compact footprint. Large windows connect directly to a private balcony, allowing natural breeze to flow in — proving that modular living can be both functional and high-quality in the busy city of Hong Kong.

Layout & Logic

Plans, Assembly & Detail

Podium & Typical Floor

The podium acts as the solid base — connecting the design to the busy Hong Kong streets while keeping the structure stable with a permanent core. It manages ground-level space, shields against strong street winds, and directs airflow toward the 12m Wind Portal.

The typical floor demonstrates the core logic: a strategic arrangement of Residential Modules, Hollow Ventilation Modules, Public Space Modules, and Staircase Modules. A mirroring strategy minimizes the variety of MiC types required for production, leading to higher construction efficiency.

Roof Terrace: The Sky Lung

The roof serves as the final component of the vertical breathing system. Programmed with community gardens and green terraces aligned with the vertical air-channels below, it acts as a thermal buffer — utilizing vegetation to reduce heat absorption while allowing rising warm air to escape freely.

At 57m, the rigid modular grid softens into a dynamic landscape of greenery and social pockets — a 'sky lung' that filters air and provides a serene escape from the high-density urban environment below.

Modular Assembly: Structure & Skin

The exploded axonometric reveals the structural anatomy of the MiC unit: a steel frame providing strength during transport and assembly, with high-performance insulation and durable external cladding attached.

By separating the structural skeleton from the wall panels, the design allows for precise manufacturing, fire resistance, weatherproofing, and long-term durability in Hong Kong's high-density urban climate.

Synthesis

A New Paradigm for
High-Density Living

The Aero-Matrix project demonstrates that high-density housing does not have to be a barrier to the urban environment. By redefining the relationship between structural efficiency and environmental performance, this design provides a scalable solution to the challenges of modern Hong Kong.

Solving the Wall Effect
Strategic Hollow Modules transform the building into a porous infrastructure that restores natural airflow.
Efficiency through MiC
High-quality assembly with minimal waste — the mirroring strategy proves technical standardization can coexist with complex spatial design.
Community Well-being
Structural gaps become communal sanctuaries — a more humane approach to compact urban living.
